Here's the uncomfortable inversion the upsell relies on: in the "regeneration booster" category, **the premium you pay and the strength of the evidence run in opposite directions.** Exosome facials sell at three to ten times a polynucleotide (Rejuran) session — on a fraction of the data. This is the piece to read before you say yes at the counter.

## Three molecules, three universes

Marketing lumps them together as "regeneration." They aren't.

- **PDRN / PN (the Rejuran family)** — salmon-DNA-derived. A defined mechanism (adenosine A2A signaling for PDRN; a viscoelastic hydrogel scaffold for the longer PN chains) with an actual wound-healing evidence trail.
- **Exosomes** — 30–150 nm vesicles carrying a signaling payload. A regenerative-medicine *story* on a cosmetic *evidence base*.

## The evidence gap, plainly

- **Exosomes:** of the human aesthetic studies to date, only a small handful are randomized; there are **zero FDA-approved therapeutic exosome products**, and regulators are actively pushing back — **Korea's MFDS banned the word "exosome" in cosmetic advertising** (effective Jan 2025). When an agency bans your marketing word, that's a verdict.
- **PDRN / PN:** modest but real — a defined pharmacology plus a wound-healing RCT floor. The aesthetic gains are incremental, not miraculous, but the *story matches the data*.

## The verdict

Pay for the evidence, not the narrative. **Exosome aesthetics is a premium-priced procedure resting on thin data and active regulatory pushback; PDRN/Rejuran is the humble booster with the stronger floor.** If a clinic leads with "stem-cell exosome regeneration," that's your cue to ask what, exactly, is in the vial — and whether anyone has measured it.
